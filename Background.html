Canada Geese & Urban Development

How human landscapes reshape wildlife observation distribution in New Jersey

Background

Canada geese can be found throughout North America, from the Arctic to Northern Mexico. They can be found in many habitats closely associated with water, including marshes, lakes and ponds.Canada geese typically feed in open grassy areas. As such, they are attracted to developed areas with well-maintained lawns such as surburban residential areas and parks. They will fly to open water to roost or to sleep at night. Their preferred food source is grass, but Canada geese will also feed on other natural food sources, including grains, aquatic plants and berries.

Canada geese have increasingly established large populations in suburban and urban environments across the northeastern United States. Unlike migratory populations, these geese thrive in human-modified landscapes where food and habitat are consistently available.

Patterns of land development play a critical role in shaping these environments. Suburban expansion introduces large areas of maintained grass, stormwater retention ponds, and fragmented wetlands— all of which provide ideal conditions for geese. As a result, developed land can support higher densities of geese than more natural or forested landscapes.

New Jersey represents an ideal case study due to its extensive suburban development and rapidly changing land cover over recent decades. This project explores how land development patterns relate to the spatial distribution and density of Canada goose observations over time.

Study Area

The map above shows the population density of New Jersey counties between the years 1980 and 2020. The highest densities are found in the northeastern part of the state, particularly in counties like Hudson and Essex, which are part of the New York metropolitan area. The southwestern counties, such as Salem and Cumberland, have much lower population densities. This distribution of human population density is a key factor influencing the distribution of Canada geese observations, as geese are more likely to be found in areas with abundant food and habitat resources provided by human development.
